Historical Development

To understand the current crisis, we must trace the evolution of machine intelligence and its relationship with human labor.

1950: The Turing Test

Alan Turing publishes "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," proposing that if a machine can mimic human responses, it is intelligent. This set the philosophical groundwork for current AI debates.

1966: ELIZA & The First Chatbots

Joseph Weizenbaum created ELIZA, a program that simulated conversation. It was the ancestor of modern LLMs, showing early on how humans easily anthropomorphize machines.

2022: The Generative Explosion

The release of ChatGPT marked a shift from "Narrow AI" (specific tasks) to "Generative AI" (creative output), disrupting schools and creative industries overnight.

Media & Persuasion

Analyzing how media frames the AI narrative and distinguishing reality from hype.

The Media Narrative

Fear vs. Hype: Media outlets often use extreme persuasion techniques. Headlines either promise a "Utopian Future" where no one has to work, or a "Robotic Apocalypse." This polarization drives clicks but obscures the nuanced reality of gradual economic shifts.

Fact vs. Inference

  • FACT: AI can process data faster than humans and pass standardized tests (Bar Exam, SATs).
  • INFERENCE: Therefore, human lawyers and teachers will become obsolete within 5 years.
  • Analysis: The inference ignores the human need for empathy, ethics, and trust which AI cannot replicate.

Proposed Solution

A creative framework to integrate AI ethically into society.

The "Augmentation Over Replacement" Protocol

Instead of banning AI, we propose a regulatory framework based on three pillars:

  1. Mandatory Disclosure: Any content (text, image, code) generated by AI must be watermarked or labeled to prevent misinformation.
  2. "Human-in-the-Loop" Laws: Critical decisions in education (grading) and employment (hiring) cannot be made solely by algorithms; a human must verify the output.
